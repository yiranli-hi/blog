I recently finished reading *Anxious People* by Swedish author Fredrik Backman.

Initially, I was drawn to the title because I believe that in today's society—rapid social change, mounting uncertainty, and the overwhelming flood of information that accompanies it ("information curse")—psychological issues have become increasingly prevalent.

This made me curious to explore what insights the book might offer about managing anxiety.

---

This is a dark humor story.

The feeling it gave me is similar to another recent read: *The Miracles of the Namiya General Store*, a lovely short story by a Japanese writer.

Both books start out somewhat suspenseful, but end up being healing and comforting.

By showing us that everyone carries their own struggles, anxieties, and challenges, the books remind us that we are not alone in our suffering.

When you feel anxious, unhappy, or burdened, please always remember that everybody feels the same.

---

## A Paragraph That Really Resonated With Me

There are many philosophically witty and humorous sentences worth bookmarking. The first paragraph that really resonated with me is this:

> Because the terrible thing about becoming an adult is being forced to realize that absolutely nobody cares about us. We must deal with everything ourselves, figure out how the entire world works. Work, pay bills, floss, attend meetings on time, stand in queues properly, fill out endless forms, untangle power cables and headphone cords, assemble furniture, change tires, charge phones, turn off coffee machines—and don't forget to enroll your children in swimming lessons.
>
> The moment we open our eyes each morning, a cascade of "don't forgets" and "remember to's" comes at us like an avalanche. We don't have time to think or breathe; we just dig through one heap knowing another will be dumped on us tomorrow.
>
> We occasionally look around—at work, at parent-teacher meetings, on the street—and are horrified to discover that everyone else seems to know exactly what they're doing. We're the only ones who have to pretend. Everyone else can afford things and has a handle on things and enough energy to deal with even more things. And everyone else's children can swim.
>
> We might never have been ready to be adults. Someone should have stopped us.
>
> When you were a child, you longed to grow up and make your own decisions. But once you grow up, you realize that "making decisions" is the most terrifying task an adult has to face.

**Yes!**

This survival stress really comes to us after becoming adults.

I do sometimes wonder:

> How exactly does everybody survive?

## Growing Up Is Not What We Imagined

When I see homeless people, I often wonder how they ended up in that situation.

When we were kids, we were told over and over again，and got tired of hearing it:

> If you don't study hard, you will go collect trash and become homeless.

It was as if everything could be solved simply by studying hard.

Well, at that time, it seemed true, because our parents protected us from everything, and studying was our only task.

We started to think that whoever got the best grades would go on to live the best life.

But when we grow up, one day, all of a sudden, we realize that studying hard does not solve many of life's problems.

Even something like finding a job—which sounded so basic and easy before—turns out to be far from easy.

How can it be so hard, right?

Everybody seems to have a job.

Well, one day, when you really start trying, you find that it is indeed very hard.

---

Those top students.

The smartest people.

The ones I thought would have the best lives as a reward for their talent and diligence.

I find that the best outcome for many of them is simply finding a decent job.

Getting a decent salary in a big company.

That's it.

Nothing more.

It offers what seems like a good salary in a big city, yet it is still so hard to afford a home there.

I've come to realize that many homeless people may have started out not so differently from everyone else.

Perhaps they struggled to find a job.

Perhaps they made some unfortunate choices, such as a failed investment.

Perhaps they lacked family support.

Or perhaps they faced mental health challenges and substance addiction.

And then the cycle begins:

Companies won't hire someone without a valid address.

Without a job, you can't afford a place to live.

## The Optimism Hidden Beneath the Anxiety

I know I'm starting to sound a little too pessimistic.

Luckily, the book ultimately adopts an optimistic stance:

> Our only understanding of the boundaries of the universe is that it has no boundaries.
>
> And regarding God, we know that we know nothing.
>
> So the pastor's demands for her family are simple: do your best.
>
> Even if we knew that tomorrow the world would go to pieces, we would still plant our apple tree today, and save what can be saved.

This reminded me of Romain Rolland's famous assertion:

> "There is only one heroism in the world: to see the world as it is, and to love it."

---

## To the End

There are so many more amazing, funny, and philosophical passages in this book.

Reading it is quiet, relaxing, and relieving.

Fredrik keeps reminding us:

Everybody is struggling.

Everybody is anxious.

Everybody is stupid.

Everyone has their own life lessons to learn.

We either succeed.

Or we get the lessons.

That sounds not too bad.
