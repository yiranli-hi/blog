*The Courage to Be Disliked* has received a lot of attention and recommendations, and even its title is very attracting. The title suggests that the book is about not caring too much about others' evaluations of us. However, the book goes beyond this idea and provides more structured guidance on how we can develop ourselves and how we can live a better life.

The book presents the psychological philosophy of Alfred Adler. He is actually regarded as one of the three major figures in psychology alongside Freud and Jung.

It starts with strongly against Freud's trauma-based psychology. Instead of explaining present suffering mainly through past trauma, Adlerian psychology emphasizes purpose, choice, and the possibility of change. Some parts of this view are debatable, and I think they need to be read critically. However, I found many other ideas in the book insightful and useful.

---

## Core Ideas

According to Adlerian psychology, our life is directed toward two major goals:

- To be self-reliant
- To live in harmony with society

More specifically, these goals are achieved by facing three major life tasks:

- The task of work
- The task of friendship
- The task of love

In the process of facing these life tasks, the solutions to the problems we may meet, or say, the psychological goals of personal growth, are:

> "I am capable." AND "Everyone is my comrade."

One of Adler's central claims is that all life issues are related to interpersonal relationship. For example, when someone does not want to go to work, it may not be because they dislike the work itself. Rather, they may fear criticism, judgment, or being labeled as "incompetent" or "unsuitable for the job." What hurts the most may not be the work, but the feeling that one's dignity and value are being hurt.

The solution, according to the book, is to believe that I am capable and to fully accept myself. The standard of evaluation should not be other people's judgment, but whether I am moving closer to the person I want to become.

This connects directly to the title of the book. *The Courage to Be Disliked* of course does not mean intentionally becoming an unpleasant person. Rather, it means having the courage to live according to one's own principles, even if some people dislike, misunderstand, or disapprove of us. Because other people's evaluations and behaviors always serve their own purposes (of course), their judgments of us are never completely neutral. This reminds me of another saying:

> How others see you is not the real you; how you see yourself is also not the real you; how you see others is the real you.

Seeking recognition from others is natural. However, if we live only to satisfy other people's expectations, we will always be controlled by them. In that sense, true freedom requires the courage to be disliked. Everyone is living for their own life and their own goals. I am the only person who can truly live for myself.

---

## Whose Task?

One important concept in the book is the "separation of tasks." The idea is that we need to distinguish between our own tasks and other people's tasks.

For example, how I act is my task. How others evaluate me is their task. I cannot control other people's opinions, because it is beyond my task.

The separation of tasks is the starting point of interpersonal relationships. However, it is not the final goal. The final goal is to develop a sense of "community feeling."

---

## Community Feeling and Social Interest

In Adlerian psychology, the ultimate goal is to see oneself as part of a larger community. This requires a shift from being overly attached to oneself to developing concern for others.

Community feeling consists of three elements:

1. **Self-acceptance** — accepting the real self
2. **Confidence in others** — trusting others without conditions
3. **Contribution to others** — feeling that I am useful to others by contribution

The concept of community in the book is very broad. It does not only refer to family, school, workplace, or local society. It can include one's country, humanity as a whole, the past and the future, and even animals, plants, and non-living things.

If all problems come from interpersonal relationships, then happiness also comes from interpersonal relationships. A person can feel happiness when they feel that they are contributing to the community.

This means that we need to actively face the tasks of interpersonal relationships. Instead of asking, "What can this person give me?" we should ask, "What can I give to this person?" By contribution, we participate in the community and develop a sense of belonging.

I think this is one of the most valuable parts of the book. Adler encourages us to look at our own value from a broader perspective. Our value does not come from defeating others in competition. It comes from whether we can contribute to the community. In this view, everyone is my comrade, rather than enemy.

---

## Inferiority and Growth

The book also discusses the feeling of inferiority. Inferiority arises because we live in society, and comparison is almost unavoidable.

However, Adler does not see inferiority itself as bad. A healthy feeling of inferiority can motivate effort and growth. There are two important aspects:

1. A healthy sense of inferiority should come from comparing myself with my ideal self, not comparing myself with others. When we are compared with others, we should remember that such comparisons are always limited to certain dimensions. Everybody is unique, and everyone has their own life path. Therefore, people as a whole are not comparable.
2. I can always make progress. As long as I am moving closer to the person I want to become, that is meaningful growth.

---

## A Debatable Point: Rejection of Trauma-Based Psychology

One of the most controversial parts of the book is its disagreement with Freud's trauma-based psychology. At the beginning of the book, it clearly opposes Freud's causal explanation of human suffering. Freud's view suggests that human psychological behavior is largely shaped by past experiences, such as trauma.

By contrast, Adlerian psychology argues that focusing on past causes is not useful. What matters more is the present purpose behind a person's behavior.

For example, the book suggests that anger may not simply be an uncontrollable emotion. Instead, anger can be used as a tool to intimidate others, gain power, or take a superior position in a relationship. It serves a purpose.

I find this perspective useful, especially when thinking about how we react to others' negative emotions. When someone reacts strongly, it may be helpful to ask: what purpose does this reaction serve? What is this person trying to achieve through this emotion?

Another valuable idea is that we are not determined by our experiences themselves, but by the meaning we give to those experiences. Even if something unfortunate happened, the way we interpret it can shape what we learn from it. The important question is not only "What was I given?" but also "How can I use what I was given?"

However, I do not fully agree with the book's strong rejection of causal explanations. I think this part needs to be understood dialectically. For people who have experienced serious trauma, external support, therapy, and care may be necessary. It may be unrealistic, and even unfair, to suggest that everything can be solved purely through personal courage or subjective agency.

In many cases, understanding the cause of one's feelings and behaviors can itself already be healing. When we know that something happened for a reason, we may feel less confused and less guilty. Understanding the cause can also bring us closer to solving the problem.

From a developmental perspective, the present is also the cause of the future. The present can be changed, and the lessons we learn from the past can be used to change the future. Therefore, I think it may be too absolute to completely reject causal explanations. A more balanced view would combine both perspectives: understanding the past while still focusing on what we can do now and how we want to move forward.

---

## Final Reflection

Overall, I appreciate the book's central message: creating value for the community instead of competing with others.

Two major principles:

> "I am capable." AND "Everyone is my comrade."

These principles can guide us toward developing the courage to be disliked.
